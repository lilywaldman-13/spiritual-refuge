An exhibition of American women & the spiritual abstract, 1918–1946

SpiritualRefuge

When the academy, the church, and the public world of ideas closed their doors, these women looked elsewhere — and found, in esoteric thought, an authority the mainstream withheld.

Five Works  ·  Pelton · Pierce · Rebay · Dreier · von Wiegand
The Argument

In the early decades of the twentieth century, American women remained excluded from most art and academic institutions, denied full participation in public religious life, and confined by social expectation to domestic and private space. There were few legitimate channels available to women through which they could exercise intellectual or creative authority. And yet, as art history has repeatedly demonstrated, the most radical innovations rarely emerge from the center, but from the edges. The most consequential visual breakthroughs of this period were being produced not in the academies and institutions of New York, but in desert studios, suburban homes, and esoteric study circles.

This exhibition argues that the exclusion of women from mainstream institutional life did not prevent their artistic development; the very disregard that shut them out also freed them to look elsewhere. Denied access to the academy, the church, and the public world of ideas, women turned to alternative systems of knowledge: Theosophy and esoteric philosophy. These were systems existing outside official hierarchies, granting women forms of authority unavailable elsewhere. Excluded from mainstream institutions, women found in esoteric study a sanctuary and an alternative center of intellectual and creative authority. These obscured systems became a sort of parallel university for women, training them to think abstractly and to see beyond the material world. This is what allowed them to develop a visual language for inner experience that conventional representation could not capture.

These works begin with the tension between domestic confinement and spiritual awakening, moving through collective esoteric circles and institutional ambition, and arriving finally at the desert studio where, freed from exclusion entirely, abstraction could reach toward the future itself. These women reveal that the spiritual and the aesthetic were inseparable, and that abstraction was the only adequate visual form for a reality they believed lay beneath the visible world. Because that form was forged under conditions of social exclusion, these women did not merely participate in American modernism; they were its foundation.

The exhibition covers roughly 1918 to 1950, a period of profound institutional and spiritual upheaval and rigidity in American life. The devastation of World War I had shaken faith in established religion and rational progress alike, and many turned to esoteric and Eastern-influenced philosophies in search of meaning that traditional institutions could no longer provide. Esoteric and metaphysical movements such as Spiritualism and Theosophy soon swept through American culture in the following decades. It would reshape how educated Americans understood consciousness, color, and the cosmos. Though for women, Theosophy offered something further still: a legitimate intellectual world in which they could lead. Women were slowly entering public life, but remained subject to powerful pressures to stay domestic, private, and subordinate. The works in this exhibition must be understood not as mystic curiosities, but as tools for interpreting a world from which institutional life had excluded them.

The Works
I
The Desert Studio — freedom in isolation

Agnes Pelton, Future

Agnes Pelton, Future, 1941. Oil on canvas.

Pelton’s Future features two stone towers floating above a desert horizon; across the picture, colored blocks and forms cut against the frame, almost looking like portals opening in a paled luminous sky, guiding the viewer from darkness toward illumination. Pelton exhibited at the famous Armory Show of 1913, though in the years that followed, the mystical abstractions she later developed remained largely dismissed by the mainstream art world of New York City. She chose instead to relocate to the barren, sparsely populated Cathedral City, California, which allowed Pelton to transcend her exclusion and immerse herself in her spiritual art and study. She would later become an honorary member of the Transcendental Painting Group, formed in New Mexico in 1938, and her practice was rooted in Theosophy, Agni Yoga, and New Thought. Future is both a record of private spiritual practice and a claim about what a painting can reflect when the artist is freed.

Spiritual systems after Michael Duncan, ed., Another World: The Transcendental Painting Group (2021).
II
The Collective — the esoteric circle

Florence Miller Pierce, Centrifics

Florence Miller Pierce, Centrifics, 1938. Oil on canvas.

Concentric geometric forms radiate and blossom outward from a center, their interlocking translucent petals intended to visually mimic energy waves or orbital paths. The animated structure guides the viewer’s eye to grow from its center — symbolizing the soul, from which the forms emerge — in a journey inward toward the psyche or the divine center of consciousness. The Transcendental Painting Group, which Pierce helped found that same year, served as a radical alternative institution, providing a sanctuary for women artists who were excluded or isolated by the patriarchal New York art establishment. The group organized itself around the belief that abstraction was a spiritual practice, drawing on Theosophy, Zen Buddhism, and Jungian psychology. For Pierce, it offered theory, community, and validation outside the official channels that remained largely closed to women working in the Southwest. Centrifics is the work of an artist who found, in spiritual community, the ability to create something mainstream practice could not.

Transcendental Painting Group context after Michael Duncan, ed., Another World (2021).
III
The Institution — building the center

Hilla Rebay, Untitled (Non-Objective Composition)

Hilla Rebay, Untitled (Non-Objective Composition), c. 1935–40. Oil on canvas.

In the 1920s, Hilla Rebay faced harsh exclusion as a female immigrant championing pure abstraction rooted in spiritual ideology within the mainstream art institutions. In response, she built her own base of power through the esoteric networks she engaged with and alternative spiritual thought. Though excluded, she remained devoted to her engagement with Theosophical ideas and spirituality and produced her own non-objective art, such as this canvas swarming with tangled geometric forms colored in contrasting green and red hues. To focus on a single point feels difficult, as stretched and sharpened shapes mercilessly cut across the canvas. This piece stands as a personal and visual exploration during a time when she herself was similarly relentless in her pursuit. In 1939 she would secure the ultimate institutional triumph by successfully advocating for the foundation of the Museum of Non-Objective Painting — later the Guggenheim — embedding a history of esoteric spirituality into a landscape that had once rejected it.

IV
The Institution — building the center

Katherine Dreier, Abstract Portrait of Marcel Duchamp

Katherine Dreier, Abstract Portrait of Marcel Duchamp, 1918. Oil on canvas.

Though trained and well-connected, Dreier found her vision and work disregarded within the art networks of New York City. She found that authority instead in Theosophy and spirituality, which held that the truest reality is invisible, and that the artist, attuned to it, could perceive and reveal what lay beneath the visible world. Dreier painted this work in 1918; rather than render Duchamp’s physical likeness, the painting uses fragmented geometric forms and dissonant color to convey the experience of his presence through a lens that feels psychic or spiritual. Spiritual insight gave her the authority her environment had long withheld, and her choice to represent Duchamp through how she experienced him is itself an assertion of that authority. Two years later she co-founded the Société Anonyme, one of the first American organizations devoted to exhibiting modern art to the public. Like Rebay, Dreier built the infrastructure of American modernism from a position of exclusion.

Theosophy and Dreier’s authority after Cynthia Fowler, “The Intersection of Theosophy and Feminism” (2000).
V
The Thesis Made Visible

Charmion von Wiegand, Ominous Form

Charmion von Wiegand, Ominous Form, 1946. Oil on canvas.

Von Wiegand’s path to spiritual abstraction began in a psychoanalyst’s office in 1927, where she admitted that what she most wanted to do was paint — a long-held desire she had been made to suppress during years of marriage and domestic life in suburban Connecticut. Her admission opened a spiritual gateway, setting in motion a decades-long transformation. Ominous Form, made in 1946, came as her engagement with Theosophy and, later, Tibetan Buddhism was deepening into the framework of her practice. Rounded, biomorphic forms interweave into a vertical column, a composition that evokes heavy restriction through a somber palette of blacks, greys, and muted whites that reflect her containment. Emerging shapes of blue sparsely spill from the edges of the form, representative of the beginning of her enlightenment into spirituality. It visualizes the tension of her life: caught between confinement, the domestic interior, and the vast spiritual systems she used to transcend it. Of all the artists here, von Wiegand makes most visible the connection between women’s social exclusion and their turn toward alternative spiritual knowledge as a source of authority, freedom, and form.

The Floorplan

The galleries unwind along a spiral. The viewer begins at the periphery — Pelton’s isolated desert studio — and moves inward through collective and institutional spaces, drawn toward the divine center the works themselves describe.
