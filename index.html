Framing Introduction

An Alternative Center of Authority

·

In the early decades of the twentieth century, American women remained excluded from most art and academic institutions, denied full participation in public religious life, and confined by social expectation to domestic and private space. There were few legitimate channels available to women through which they could exercise intellectual or creative authority. And yet, as art history has repeatedly demonstrated, the most radical innovations rarely emerge from the center, but from the edges. The most consequential visual breakthroughs of this period were being produced not in the academies and institutions of New York, but in desert studios, suburban homes, and esoteric study circles.

This exhibition argues that the exclusion of women from mainstream institutional life did not prevent their artistic development; the very disregard that shut them out also freed them to look elsewhere. Denied access to the academy, the church, and the public world of ideas, women turned to alternative systems of knowledge: Theosophy and esoteric philosophy.1 These were systems existing outside official hierarchies, granting women forms of authority unavailable elsewhere. Excluded from mainstream institutions, women found in esoteric study a sanctuary and an alternative center of intellectual and creative authority. These obscured systems became a sort of parallel university for women, training them to think abstractly and to see beyond the material world. This is what allowed them to develop a visual language for inner experience that conventional representation could not capture.

These works begin with the tension between domestic confinement and spiritual awakening, moving through collective esoteric circles and institutional ambition, and arriving finally at the desert studio where, freed from exclusion entirely, abstraction could reach toward the future itself. These women reveal that the spiritual and the aesthetic were inseparable, and that abstraction was the only adequate visual form for a reality they believed lay beneath the visible world.2 Because that form was forged under conditions of social exclusion, these women did not merely participate in American modernism; they were its foundation.

The exhibition covers roughly 1910 to 1950, a period of profound institutional and spiritual upheaval and rigidity in American life. The devastation of World War I had shaken faith in established religion and rational progress alike, and many turned to esoteric and Eastern-influenced philosophies in search of meaning that traditional institutions could no longer provide. Esoteric and metaphysical movements such as Spiritualism and Theosophy soon swept through American culture in the following decades. It would reshape how educated Americans understood consciousness, color, and the cosmos.3 Though for women, Theosophy offered something further still: a legitimate intellectual world in which they could lead. Women were slowly entering public life, but remained subject to powerful pressures to stay domestic, private, and subordinate. The works in this exhibition must be understood not as mystic curiosities, but as tools for interpreting a world from which institutional life had excluded them.

Five Works · Following the Spiral Inward

The Exhibition

·
I
Charmion von Wiegand, Ominous Form, 1946. Oil on canvas.
I · Domestic Confinement & Awakening

Ominous Form 1946

Charmion von Wiegand (1896–1983)
Object Label

Rounded, biomorphic forms interweave into a vertical column, built from a somber palette of blacks, greys, and muted whites that registers as containment. Sparse spills of blue escape the form's edges — the first signs of a spiritual awakening. Von Wiegand's path to abstraction began in a psychoanalyst's office in 1927, where she admitted that what she most wanted to do was paint, a desire long suppressed in suburban married life. Ominous Form visualizes the tension between domestic interior and the vast spiritual systems she used to transcend it. Of all the artists here, she makes most visible the link between women's exclusion and their turn toward alternative spiritual knowledge as a source of authority.

II
Florence Miller Pierce, Centrifics, 1938. Oil on canvas.
II · The Esoteric Circle

Centrifics 1938

Florence Miller Pierce (1918–2007)
Object Label

Concentric geometric forms radiate and blossom outward from a luminous center, mimicking energy waves or orbital paths and drawing the eye toward a core that symbolizes the soul — the journey inward to the divine center of consciousness. That same year, Pierce helped found the Transcendental Painting Group, a radical alternative institution that gave sanctuary to artists excluded by the patriarchal New York establishment. Organized around the belief that abstraction was a spiritual practice, the group drew on Theosophy, Zen Buddhism, and Jungian psychology. Centrifics is the work of an artist who found, in spiritual community, the theory, validation, and freedom that mainstream channels denied her.

III
Hilla Rebay, Untitled (Non-Objective Composition), c. 1935–40. Oil on canvas.
III · Institutional Ambition

Untitled c. 1935–40

Hilla Rebay (1890–1967)
Object Label

A canvas swarming with tangled geometric forms in contrasting green and red, where stretched and sharpened shapes cut relentlessly across the surface, refusing the eye a single resting point. In the 1920s, Rebay faced harsh exclusion as a female immigrant championing pure, spiritually rooted abstraction within mainstream institutions. In response she built her own base of power through esoteric networks and Theosophical thought, producing non-objective work like this during a period when she herself was equally relentless in her pursuit. In 1939 she secured the ultimate institutional triumph — advocating successfully for the Museum of Non-Objective Painting, embedding esoteric spirituality into a landscape that had once rejected it.

IV
Katherine Dreier, Abstract Portrait of Marcel Duchamp, 1918. Oil on canvas.
IV · The Reach Toward Authority

Abstract Portrait of Marcel Duchamp 1918

Katherine S. Dreier (1877–1952)
Object Label

Rather than render Duchamp's physical likeness, Dreier builds his portrait from fragmented geometric forms and dissonant color, conveying the experience of his presence through a lens that feels psychic or spiritual. Though trained and well-connected, she found her vision disregarded within the art networks of New York, and located authority instead in Theosophy and spirituality — which held that the truest reality is invisible, and that the attuned artist could reveal what lies beneath the visible world. Her choice to depict Duchamp through how she experienced him is itself an assertion of that authority. Like Rebay, Dreier built the infrastructure of American modernism from a position of exclusion.

V
Agnes Pelton, Future, 1941. Oil on canvas.
V · The Desert Studio & the Future

Future 1941

Agnes Pelton (1881–1961)
Object Label

Two stone towers float above a desert horizon; colored blocks cut against the frame like portals opening in a pale, luminous sky, guiding the viewer from darkness toward illumination. Pelton exhibited at the famous Armory Show of 1913, yet the mystical abstractions she later developed were largely dismissed by the New York art world. She relocated instead to the barren, sparsely populated Cathedral City, California, where she could transcend her exclusion and immerse herself in spiritual study, drawing on Theosophy, Agni Yoga, and New Thought; she became an honorary member of the Transcendental Painting Group. Future is at once a record of private spiritual practice and a claim about what a painting can reflect when the artist is finally free.

Floorplan & Sequence

A Spiral Drawn Inward

·

The gallery is laid out as a single spiral. Visitors enter at the wide outer edge and follow the curve inward, retracing the journey the works themselves describe — from von Wiegand's domestic confinement, through the esoteric circles of Pierce and the institution-building of Rebay and Dreier, toward the still center anticipated by Pelton's Future. Scale and proximity tighten as the path winds in, so that the viewer's body enacts the inward turn "toward the divine center of consciousness" that the paintings depict.
